The beloved tale of Star Wars is one that almost didn't happen. Countless stories that we could have been robbed of without ever even knowing it. Generations may never have known about the tales of the Skywalker family.

This is the tale of how Star Wars almost didn't happen.

After the premiere of his first feature length film, THX 1138, George Lucas began to explore the possibility of a Space Western. He wanted to base it on Flash Gordon, which he loved as a child. He was never able to secure the rights to it so he decided to write his own space fantasy.

He began developing his Space Opera, which he would call “The Star Wars” while he worked on his next movie, American Grafitti. After American Graffiti premiered, he began to focus his attention on Star Wars writing non stop as ideas continued to flow.

He borrowed ideas from Tarzan movies, Jules Verne, Kurusawa films, and even The Wizard of Oz in order to create his space opera. He finished his 13 page treatment in April of 1973 and began to shop around for a studio to financially back it.

After being rejected by United Artists, Universal Studios, and Walt Disney Studios, Lucas and his producer, Gary Kurtz, shopped it to 20th Century Fox. They head of the studio, Alan Ladd, didn’t think much of the story, but he saw talent in George Lucas and decided to financially back it based on that faith. By June, the deal was signed. Star Wars would be backed.

Over the next year, Lucas would rewrite the treatment four times before fleshing out the ideas. He has said that “searching for just the right ingredients, characters and storyline. It's always been what you might call a good idea in search of a story.”

He continued to write and in January of 1975, it began to shape up into the Star Wars we would eventually come to know. The Force was developed as a mystical energy field that Jedi and Sith could tap into. Characters were developed although appearances weren’t yet nailed down.

Lucas hired conceptual artist Ralph McQuarrie to create paintings of certain scenes at this time. When Lucas delivered the screenplay to the studio, he included these paintings.

20th century Fox approved a budget of $8.25 million and, due to the success of American Graffiti, allowed Lucas to keep the rights to any sequels and merchandising. The studio thought the movie would do all right but didn’t think much of a future for the Star Wars.

George Lucas would form his own visual effects company after learning that Fox’s had been disbanded. This company is Industrial Light and Magic. ILM would pioneer and create it’s own technology in order to achieve the shots that George Lucas wanted.

Kurtz initially scouted out the Phillipines as a location, as Lucas wanted Tatooine to be a jungle planet. After Lucas worried about filming in the jungle, he refined his idea to make Tatooine the desert planet we now know and Kurtz found Tunisia as the perfect location.

Casting began and many relatively unknown people would soon shoot up to stardom. Mark Hamill had a sitcom but would skyrocket after Star Wars. Carrie Fisher was known but not by anything important. Harrison Ford had minor roles in films but was primarily a carpenter. He would eventually become the biggest star of all of them.

After Geoffrey Unsworth dropped out of his contract to be the cinematographer for Star Wars, Lucas hired Gilbert Taylor because he was impressed by his work on Dr Strangelove. After filming began, there was an immediate clash in ideals as to how the film should be shot.

Lucas came up as an independent filmmaker and was used to doing many things on his own. Taylor objected to Lucas’s decisions. Lucas would tell him he wanted something lit a certain way and Taylor would deny him that. He told Lucas that he would do things the way he wanted, which is a slap in the face to any filmmaker.

Star Wars would face other problems during filming. Props and equipment would break almost as a routine and there was an extremely rare thunderstorm that happened during the first week of filming. All of this threatened to derail the project. Fox executives were getting angry that they haven’t seen a return on their investment yet.

After Tunisia was finished, the production moved to Elstree Studios, where the sets would be built for the Death Star. The studio workers didn’t care about the project and did things very half-ass. They took breaks when they felt like it and joked around about the movie being a kids show. Taylor began to shine by creating the in-wall lighting system because everything was so dark.

Ladd remained the only executive at Fox Studios that supported the project, even pushing for a higher budget. Executives were pushy, production was messy, and equipment was faulty. Lucas was diagnosed with hypertension and exhaustion. His doctor warned him that if he kept going like this, he would have a heart attack. Lucas had to keep going though. His vision was getting close.

Star Wars was initially supposed to be released for Christmas of 1976 but the post-production process and continued butting of heads set the film date back to Summer of 1977.

During post-production, the editor, John Jympson, decided not to cut the film the way Lucas wanted. He did things his own way and the movie looked terrible. Lucas replaced him when he refused to recut it and hired Paul Hirsch and Richard Chew to recut it. He even had his then-wife, Marcia Lucas, recut some scenes.

In February of 1977, a very rough version of the film was shown to select directors and Fox executives. The special effects were unfinished and it was met with very mixed reactions. Steven Spielberg was the only director who enjoyed the film but felt the lukewarm reaction of others was due to the unfinished special effects. Fox executives liked the version but increased the budget of the film to 11 million so that Lucas could finish the special effects.

ILM created their own technologies to finish the effects. Stop motion effects, optical tricks, and green screen rigs were developed and used to make these shots work right. The production was finished mere weeks before the release date of May 25, 1977.

The stress was well worth it and Star Wars launched into an international phenomenon. It is a household name and has been the source material of seven movies, with four more in various stages of development, multiple TV shows, videogames, comics, books, toys, and every other possible merchandise you can think of. Very few people believed in this vision but Lucas was determined and it skyrocketed him to success.
